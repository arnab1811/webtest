SEED ECOLOGY

 

 

Seed ecology is the study of the ecological strategies plants use to ensure their reproduction by seed1. These strategies include:

  • Timing of seed production: Plants may produce seeds at different times of the year or in different years, depending on environmental factors such as temperature, rainfall, day length, and pollinator availability2.
  • Allocation of nutrients to seeds: Plants may produce few or many seeds, big or small ones, depending on their life history traits and trade-offs between seed quantity and quality3. Larger seeds usually have higher survival and growth rates than smaller seeds, but they are more costly to produce and disperse.
  • Mechanisms of seed dispersal: Plants may rely on various agents such as wind, water, animals, or gravity to disperse their seeds away from the parent plant and reduce competition and predation. Some plants have specialized structures or adaptations to facilitate seed dispersal, such as wings, hairs, hooks, spines, or fleshy fruits.
  • Understanding the seed bank: The seed bank is the reservoir of viable seeds that are present in the soil or on the plant surface. The seed bank can influence plant population dynamics, community composition, and succession by providing a source of regeneration after disturbances or environmental changes.
  • Environmental conditions that impose dormancy and induce germination: Dormancy is a state of reduced metabolic activity that prevents seeds from germinating under unfavorable conditions. Dormancy can be broken by various environmental cues such as light, temperature, moisture, oxygen, fire, or chemicals. Germination is the process of seed activation and emergence of the radicle (embryonic root).
  • Factors that influence successful seedling establishment: Seedling establishment is the transition from a germinating seed to a photosynthetic plant that can survive and grow independently. Seedling establishment depends on factors such as seed size, seedling vigor, soil properties, biotic interactions, and disturbance regimes.

Seed ecology is important for understanding plant evolution, diversity, distribution, and conservation. It also has applications in agriculture, forestry, horticulture, restoration ecology, and weed management.
